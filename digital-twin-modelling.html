Bridging the digital and physical worlds with digital twin technology

A digital twin is a digital representation of a physical object, process, or service. A digital twin can be a digital replica of an object in the physical world, such as a jet engine or organ in the human body or wind farms, or even larger items such as buildings or even whole cities.

Creating digital twin requires applied mathematics and data science expertise. WNL engineers combine the physics and operational data of target object or system to develop a mathematical model that simulates the original. They build digital twins by making use of real-world data to create a computer simulation that models the product or process and ensures that the virtual model can connect with sensors that gather data from the real world. Digital twin is a real-time simulation of the real-life version, enabling insights into performance and potential problems. These twins can be integrated with Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), and software analytics to solve challenging problems.

Digital twin complexity varies based on the amount of data, determining how precisely the model simulates the real world physical version. The twin can be used as a prototype to offer feedback on the product as it is developed or model what could occur with a physical version when built.

There are three major types of Digital twins:

  • Digital Twin Prototype (DTP) – Created before a physical product is created

  • Digital Twin Instance (DTI) – Created after a product is ready to test usage scenarios

  • Digital Twin Aggregate (DTA) – Collects DTI data for evaluating product performance & operation

Digital twins are being used in a wide variety of industries for a range of applications and purposes. In Manufacturing to create efficient design models and improving maintenance and upgrades that reduce production times significantly; in Automotive virtual models of connected vehicle, capture behavioral and operational data resulting in a personalized experience for car owners; that can be customized to their specific needs; in Retail virtual models of stores augment the customer experience; in Medical organ models are being used in training and de-risking of procedures; in Disaster Management , models of cities, infrastructure and buildings can be used to manage emergency response; and in Smart Cities digital twin are helping cities become more economically, environmentally and socially sustainable by guiding planning decisions and offering solutions to the many complex challenges faced by modern cities.

SCDT (Smart City Digital Twin)

Smart city digital twin is a multi-layer model about the terrain, buildings, infrastructure, mobility, and IoT devices. Information from simulations in the SCDT can be used to test new scenarios through the layers of the model and then implemented in the physical world

Expanding deployment of ICT, sensors, and the IoT collect data about pretty much every movement, flow, or activity in a city. This data, combined with cloud computing and AI can be used for city planning, scenario planning, zoning and development. City planners can factor in local climate resilience, economic development, housing goals etc.
Decision support systems using SCDT can foster collaboration between planning organizations and other city departments and agencies. SCDT can serve as the site where all city data lives; environmental indicators, resource consumption, zoning regulations, total housing units, transportation patterns. Standardized data from multiple areas in a single repository can be shared across their various initiatives and programs, promoting cross-departmental experimentation that better understands interdependencies between policies and infrastructure systems. SCDT can be help cities model and deploy fast and effective responses to natural and man-made disasters, manage risks, and rapidly create recovery plans. Digital twins can help stakeholders visualize projects and developments, even in remote situations.
